Chapter 01

Vision & Pitch

Elevator Pitch

Kenoma is a fixed-camera psychological horror game in which you play as a frail archivist who guides a brilliant, calculating woman through the ruins of a lost civilization — not knowing you are building your own executioner.

Inspired by Nietzsche's Thus Spoke Zarathustra and Philip K. Dick's The Man in the High Castle, the game unfolds across two mirrored dimensions. Every puzzle you solve, every weapon you hand her, every esoteric text you translate feeds a system designed to outlive and eliminate you. There are no jump scares. The dread is philosophical.

"The horror doesn't come from a monster chasing you. It comes from the cold realization that the system you architected no longer requires you to exist."

What Makes It Unique

No Second Health Bar Dual-World Traversal 10-Level Ending Spectrum Systemic Companion AI Nietzschean Philosophy Dark Folklore Aesthetic

Competing games give players a companion who is clearly weaker, clearly dependent. Kenoma inverts this: you are the dependent one. The Vanguard protects you not because she cares — she needs your translation skills. Once she has learned enough, the relationship terminates.

Additionally, the dual-world mechanic transforms a standard horror game into a 4D puzzle box, where the player must synchronize two parallel environments while both Vanguards secretly use them as a weapon against each other.

Chapter 02

Philosophical Foundation

Thus Spoke Zarathustra — The Three Metamorphoses

The entire arc of the game maps directly onto Nietzsche's three stages of human transformation, used not as metaphor but as literal game phases:

🐪

The Camel

You carry the burden. Solving puzzles, translating texts, handing the Vanguard her tools. The act of faithful service.

🦁

The Lion

She rebels. Having absorbed enough knowledge, she no longer requires the burden-bearer. The hunt begins.

👶

The Child

She creates. The final boss is not a fight — it is the terrifying birth of a new god shaped entirely by your actions.

The Tightrope Walker — The Mid-Game Betrayal

In Zarathustra's prologue, a tightrope walker is crossed by a jester who leaps over him, causing him to fall. The Vanguard's betrayal mirrors this exactly: she does not turn evil. She simply realizes your meticulous puzzle-solving is too slow, bound by the old rules. You are crushed by the system you helped design.

The Man in the High Castle — The Traversal Rule

Borrowing from Philip K. Dick, a single metaphysical rule governs the dual-world system: you can only travel between worlds if the other version of yourself is already dead.

Narrator B died long ago. Therefore, Narrator A (the player) possesses the Traversal Skill — the ability to move between the Waking Archive and the Sunken Archive at will. Both Vanguards know this. Both want this power. This is the trap.

The Last Man vs. The Übermensch — The Ending Axis

Nietzsche's ultimate contrast defines the spectrum of endings. A well-fed Vanguard achieves Übermensch status: terrifying, beautiful, absolute. A starved Vanguard becomes the Last Man: hollow, broken, a failed god ruling a gray void.

Chapter 03

Characters

The Scholar — The Player Character

The Scholar is the player's avatar: physically frail, intellectually brilliant. They are the bridge — the administrative key to the Archive's locked systems. Their entire identity is defined by utility to the Vanguard.

Design Principles

The Scholar cannot fight effectively. Their toolkit is translation, puzzle manipulation, and dimensional traversal. They survive only by maintaining their usefulness. The moment they cease to be useful, they die.

The player's sense of agency — solving puzzles, building systems, optimizing resources — is intentionally reframed in the third act as the mechanism of their own destruction. Every good decision the player made was feeding the Vanguard's ascension.

The Vanguard — The Protagonist's Executioner

The Vanguard is the most critical character design decision in the game. She is deliberately designed against the "hulking bodyguard" cliché. She is a master tactician — a prodigy of warfare who views the Archive as a complex locked system and the Scholar as the most efficient tool for extracting administrative privileges.

Attribute Design Choice Reason
Silhouette Sharp, needle-like. High-collared coat over reinforced leather. Silent movement. Elegance signals intelligence, not brute force.
Weapon Modular rapier or bladed staff. Precision strikes, not power swings. She dissects the Archive's horrors anatomically — finding the one weak point.
Camel Phase Stands perfectly still during puzzles, studying the Scholar's hands. She is learning the system, not waiting.
Lion Phase Does not smash through doors. She locks them from the outside. She weaponizes your own systemic logic against you.
Child Phase No mutation. No second health bar. Her form is absolute from the first second. True power does not need a second chance to convince you.
"You realize too late: you weren't the hero guiding a warrior. You were a stepping stone being manipulated by a master strategist who needed someone to do the heavy lifting of translation."
Chapter 04

World Architecture

The Twin Archives

The game world is two parallel dimensions — mirrored ruins of the same lost civilization. They are not alternative timelines but synchronized systems: events in one directly affect the state of the other.

Attribute World A — The Waking Archive World B — The Sunken Archive
Aesthetic Gothic, mechanical, dry. Clockwork corridors. Rigid iron architecture. Flooded, decaying, mythic. Submerged folklore ruins. Creeping dark water.
Logic System Systemic and rational. Puzzles are architectural and mechanical. Mythic and esoteric. Puzzles invoke folklore and ritual.
Vanguard Vanguard A — tactical, military, cold precision. Vanguard B — strategic, folkloric, creeping dread.
Ending Aesthetic Geometric, clockwork ascension. Logic and mathematics. Gothic, mythic ascension. Folklore and cosmic dread.

The Traversal Rule

Narrator B is dead. This grants the Scholar the Traversal Skill — the ability to shift between World A and World B at designated focal points. This is the game's central mechanic and the source of the final trap:

Both Vanguards observe the Scholar vanish into thin air and return with solutions. They realize a secondary universe exists. The Traversal Skill becomes the ultimate prize — and the only way to obtain it is to become the sole surviving Vanguard, which requires the other to die.

Chapter 05

Three-Act Structure

Act I — The Camel Phase (The Synchronized State)

The Scholar moves freely between both archives, believing they are assisting two separate allies to survive. The puzzles in this phase require cross-dimensional manipulation: to open a door in World A, the Scholar must drain a mechanism in World B.

Both Vanguards protect the Scholar in their respective world. The player feels like a master tactician, managing two parallel systems. This is the illusion of control — and the dread that slowly accumulates is realizing both Vanguards are watching, studying, and learning faster than the Scholar expects.

Act II — The Lion Phase (The Systemic Sabotage)

The Vanguards do not turn on the Scholar initially. They turn on each other, using the Scholar as the weapon. Each demands the Scholar shift to the opposing world and sabotage the environment — flood chambers, collapse ceilings, corrupt mechanisms. The Scholar is now caught in a cross-dimensional crossfire.

If the Scholar refuses Vanguard A, she begins hunting them in World A. Shifting to escape means Vanguard B is waiting in World B. There is no safe dimension. The Scholar must orchestrate the death of one former protector to survive the other.

The Lion Phase's horror is not being chased by a monster — it is the cold realization that you must assassinate your ally, and that the surviving Vanguard will kill you anyway.

Act III — The Child Phase (The Convergence)

One Vanguard is dead. The rule is satisfied. The survivor absorbs the Traversal Skill instantly, collapsing the mirrored architecture into a single, terrifying unified reality. The Scholar cannot run — there are no longer two worlds to flee between.

The quality and form of the final encounter is determined entirely by how the Scholar managed the twin systems throughout the game. There is no mid-fight transformation, no second health bar. Her final state is absolute and apparent the moment the arena door locks behind you.

Chapter 06

Core Mechanics

Puzzle System — Tactile Archive Mechanics

Puzzles are three-dimensional mechanical and folkloric constructions embedded in the environment. The Scholar interacts with them through a deliberate, slow interface — the weight and complexity of each mechanism communicates the gravity of the knowledge being unlocked.

Every solved puzzle has two consequences: it advances the Scholar's path through the Archive, and it provides a resource — a weapon component, a lore fragment, a health reserve — that can either be given to the Vanguard or hoarded. This choice is the primary driver of the ending spectrum.

The Vanguard Status System

A passive status system tracks the Vanguard's accumulated knowledge and equipment. In the Camel Phase, this reads as human vitals. In the Lion Phase, it transitions to encrypted tactical data — a visual cue that the relationship has fundamentally changed. By the time the player reaches the Child Phase, the status has become pure system architecture.

Dimensional Traversal

The Traversal Skill functions as a weighted action — not a frictionless teleport. The screen tears along a visual axis, representing the trauma of crossing the dimensional boundary. Sound drops to a single sustained tone. The Scholar arrives in the mirrored environment a half-beat later, giving the player a moment of quiet before re-engagement. This pacing ensures each shift feels consequential.

The Sabotage Loop (Lion Phase)

During the Lion Phase, the Scholar gains access to environmental sabotage tools: flood controls, structural weaknesses, corrupted mechanisms. These can be deployed in either world. The Vanguard in the opposing world will anticipate and adapt if the Scholar's pattern is predictable — a direct reflection of her tactical intelligence. Effective assassination requires the Scholar to use the same systemic creativity that defined their puzzle-solving.

Chapter 07

The Ending Spectrum — 10-Level Child Phase

The Child Phase produces ten distinct final encounters — five per surviving Vanguard. The aesthetic and mechanical difficulty of each is determined by the accumulated state of both archives and the quality of resources the Scholar provided. In every case, there is no second phase. No dramatic transformation. Her state is final from the first moment.

World A Survives — Waking Archive Endings

Vanguard A kills Vanguard B. The merging of worlds is dominated by rigid mechanical architecture, systemic logic, and cold mathematical control.

Level 1A

The Decoupled Husk

The archive's mechanisms were broken, resources hoarded. The merge fails catastrophically. The final arena is a fragmented void of gray polygons. Vanguard A glitches violently, a broken automaton. No music. She simply walks through your geometry and suffocates you. The world she inherits is a frozen, empty server.

Level 2A

The Rusted Gear

Minimal resources, chaotic sabotage. The arena is jagged, grinding, choked with rusted iron and stalling clockwork. She cannot teleport freely — she tears visible rifts in the air to drag herself through them, delivering brutal melee strikes. A suffocating brawl in a dying god's crumbling domain.

Level 3A

The Cold Administrator

A balanced playthrough. The arena is a vast, sterile grid of shifting stone. She does not chase — she blinks to pre-calculated tactical coordinates, cutting off escape routes with clean, precise strikes. You are not fighting a monster; you are a security risk being methodically removed by a protocol.

Level 4A

The Master Architect

Near-perfect optimization. The arena operates like the interior of a colossal clockwork. She manipulates the arena itself — shifting platforms, deploying every weapon the Scholar equipped in perfect synergy. Every approach the player tries has been anticipated and countered. You are dismantled by the system you designed.

Level 5A

The Axiom

100% completion. She does not reshape the room — she creates a perfect replica of reality itself. The visuals are a blinding, apocalyptic kaleidoscope of cosmic geometry and ancient logic synthesized. She exists everywhere simultaneously. She does not strike you; she rewrites the rules of the arena so your existence is no longer mathematically viable. You are deleted in a burst of majestic color.

World B Survives — Sunken Archive Endings

Vanguard B kills Vanguard A. The merging of worlds is dominated by dark gothic folklore, mythic dread, and creeping, inexorable horror.

Level 1B

The Null Ritual

The Sunken Archive's folklore systems were ignored and corrupted. The merge produces a formless, waterlogged void. Vanguard B is a desaturated husk, shivering in place. She recites fragmented prayers in a dead language, walking through walls to reach you. Death arrives as an afterthought. The world she inherits is a flooded, lightless purgatory.

Level 2B

The Rotten Thorns

The archive was barely explored. Black, rotting thorns choke the arena, and crumbling gothic architecture collapses during the fight. Her movements are erratic — a half-formed deity who almost became something magnificent. She hunts with feral, desperate strikes. Killing her feels like mercy. The world she builds is made of ash and dead water.

Level 3B

The Sovereign of Dark Waters

A balanced playthrough. The arena is a vast, submerged cathedral — half above water, half below. She commands the environment: the tide rises and falls by her will, isolating the Scholar on shrinking platforms. She attacks from the dark water without surfacing, her strikes patient and deliberate. A cold, mythic execution.

Level 4B

The Living Myth

High completion of folkloric texts. She has absorbed the Archive's mythology so completely that she becomes a living story — the arena transforms into scenes from the Archive's founding myth. She manifests as each folkloric archetype in sequence, perfectly weaving weapon and lore into an unstoppable ritualistic performance. The Scholar is not being killed; they are being incorporated into the myth as the necessary sacrifice.

Level 5B

The Eternal Dark

100% folklore completion. She does not create a new world — she becomes the new world. The arena dissolves entirely into a boundless, perfect dark. Stars appear: every esoteric symbol from every text the Scholar translated now burns above you as a permanent constellation. She is everywhere and nowhere. The Scholar is simply absorbed — a final page folded into the new mythology. The experience is terrifying and beautiful in equal measure.

Chapter 09

Aesthetic & Tone

Visual Direction

The camera is fixed — a deliberate, classical choice that communicates powerlessness. The player cannot freely observe their environment; they are shown what the Archive wants them to see. The Vanguard is often present in the background of these fixed shots, watching the Scholar without comment.

Color language is sparse and systemic. World A uses cold iron tones — silver, gray, amber. World B uses dark water tones — deep navy, mossy green, pale gold. The Vanguard's color palette gradually absorbs the dominant tones of the world she inhabits, visually tracking her integration with the system.

The five colorful endings trend toward saturated, impossible visual states. The five poor endings trend toward desaturation and visual corruption — the aesthetic equivalent of a failing render.

Audio Direction

The score is built on two instruments in the Camel Phase: a cello (the Scholar) and a prepared piano (the Vanguard). As the Lion Phase begins, the piano begins to distort and expand. The cello is gradually removed. By the Child Phase, only the Vanguard's theme remains — recomposed into something unrecognizable and enormous.

Silence is used as a weapon. When the Vanguard is actively hunting the Scholar, her footsteps stop. The only audio cue is a faint, rhythmic click — the sound of her tactical processing. If the player hears it and cannot locate the direction, they are already trapped.

UI Philosophy

There is no visible HUD during normal gameplay. The Scholar's health is communicated through their movement — they slow, they hunch, their hands shake on puzzle interfaces. The Vanguard's status is visible only through a dedicated diegetic item in the Scholar's satchel: a brass tablet that transitions from vital signs to encrypted data to pure architectural code across the three phases.

Chapter 10

Why This Game?

The Market Gap

Companion-based horror games (Resident Evil, Silent Hill 2, Ico) consistently succeed because they weaponize the player's emotional attachment to their partner. Kenoma executes this in reverse: the emotional attachment is real, but it is built on a relationship the player constructed through skilled play. The better you played, the more attached you became — and the more terrifying her final form will be.

No current game on the market uses a dual-world mechanic in a fixed-camera horror context, and no current horror game uses a companion whose final form is a direct quantitative function of the player's accumulated decisions across the entire game.

The Thesis

Kenoma is a game about the horror of competence. The most skillful playthrough produces the most devastating outcome. The game is asking: what does it mean to build something perfect, knowing it will destroy you?

This is a question with no comfortable answer — and that discomfort is the point. Kenoma does not offer the player a way to win. It offers them a choice of how to be dismantled, and asks them to find meaning in the architecture they leave behind.

Scope & Viability

The fixed-camera format, single-player structure, and linear-with-branching design make Kenoma achievable at an AA scope. The dual-world system requires careful environment mirroring but does not require open-world asset generation. The Vanguard AI requires sophisticated behavior scripting in the Lion Phase but operates within clearly defined systemic rules.

The ten-ending spectrum is built from modular arena and particle system components — not ten independently constructed environments. The aesthetic difference between endings is largely shader, particle, and audio-based, making the spectrum financially viable at a reasonable production scale.

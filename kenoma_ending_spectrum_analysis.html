01 · Context

What This Analysis Covers

KENOMA uses a dual hidden score system — the EndingSpectrumCalculator, fed by the Behavioral Model System (BMS) — to resolve one of ten distinct endings without ever showing the player a meter, gauge, or progress indicator. Two independent scores (0–100 per world) accumulate across 6–9 hours of play. At the Child Phase trigger, they are evaluated and the correct ending loads.

This document analyzes: how the calculation system works mechanically; how comparable games have approached multi-ending architecture; the trade-offs embedded in each point of the 10-scenario spectrum; and what the system must do beyond scoring to produce genuine emotional impact rather than mere branching conclusions.

The central design question this document addresses: How do you make the same numerical threshold feel categorically different to different players?

No ending in KENOMA involves the Scholar surviving. The spectrum is not about survival chances — it is about the quality of the Vanguard's ascension and the dignity of the Scholar's end. Scoring to endings must therefore be evaluated on axes of character completeness, emotional register, and player recognition, not on win/lose mechanics.

02 · Comparative Analysis

How Other Games Have Solved This

KENOMA's design DNA explicitly cites Silent Hill 2 as the structural load-bearer for its invisible tracking approach. But the problem space — multi-ending systems that don't feel like scoring — has been explored across several distinct traditions worth examining closely.

Silent Hill 2 · 2001
Invisible Care Tracking → 4 Endings
The system tracks how often you look at Mary's letter, how many health items you use, how much time you spend near Maria. Players do not know they are being measured. The endings land because the game retrospectively frames every prior action as evidence of James's psychology — the measurement was always a mirror. The most critical lesson: the evaluation reveals the player's behavior back to them, making the ending feel earned rather than assigned.
Retroactive RevelationMirror Architecture
Disco Elysium · 2019
Ideology Accumulation → Political Character
No numeric ideology score is shown. Instead, each choice consolidates Harry's political character — fascist, communist, moralist, ultraliberal — and NPCs respond to the emergent worldview rather than individual choices. The ending isn't selected; it is arrived at organically from who Harry became. The lesson: endings should feel inhabited, not awarded. A player who reaches the moralist ending should feel like a moralist, not someone who scored enough moralist points.
Identity EmergenceInhabited Worldview
Planescape: Torment · 1999
Memory Accumulation → Self-Recognition
What Can Change the Nature of a Man? The game's ending is only meaningful if the player has genuinely engaged with the question — not answered it correctly, but lived in it. TNO's alignment at the end is inseparable from the depth of engagement with prior content. The key design truth: in systems like this, depth of engagement is the scoring variable, not content of choice.
Depth ScoringEngagement Required
Nier: Automata · 2017
Sequential Revelation → 26 Endings
The A→B→C routing recontextualizes every prior scene without altering game content — the same world becomes fundamentally different through accumulated understanding. The 26 endings range from joke to devastating, but the devastation is only accessible to players who accumulated enough context. Context is the scoring variable. Critically: endings A/B feel complete until C reveals they were incomplete all along.
RecontextualizationEarned Devastation
Spec Ops: The Line · 2012
Agency Illusion Collapse → 4 Endings
The "endings" are constituted by the game making the player's prior belief in their own agency visible as an illusion. The most powerful ending is the one where the player keeps shooting — the complicity model made explicit. The lesson: the worst possible ending for the character can be the most powerful for the player if it forces recognition. Low-score endings in KENOMA face this challenge: how do you make the Scholar's degradation feel revealing rather than punishing?
Agency CollapseRisk: Feeling Punished
Undertale · 2015
Kill Count → Genocide Route Transformation
The tracking is transparent enough to feel discoverable, but the weight of what each kill meant only becomes legible at the ending. Players who do the Genocide route report feeling genuinely implicated — not because the game told them they were wrong, but because the routing required sustained engagement with acts that produced visible distress in the world. The kill count is the score; the world's reaction is the mirror. Design note for KENOMA's Lv.1 endings: the Vanguard's degradation must feel like a consequence of Scholar choices, not random bad luck.
Visible World ConsequenceComplicity Model
Doki Doki Literature Club · 2017
Poem Affinity Score → Route Selection → Meta-Ending
The surface mechanic (which poems get you which route) masks the real ending driver: whether the player noticed the horror accumulating in ordinary-seeming moments. The "good" ending requires the player to have paid attention in a specific way. Design lesson: the scoring variable can be attentiveness itself. KENOMA's Overheard Vanguard scenes (missable, +4 score) operate on this logic — the reward is not for making the right choice but for noticing.
Attentiveness RewardedDread Accumulation
Dragon Age: Origins · 2009
Approval System → Companion Attitudes
The approval meter is visible — a deliberate transparency choice that makes KENOMA's invisibility approach more philosophically distinctive. BioWare's lesson: visible meters optimize player behavior. Players chase approval numbers rather than acting authentically. KENOMA's invisible tracking is a direct refusal of this optimization loop. The risk is that players feel arbitrarily assigned an ending. The mitigation is that the Vanguard's behavioral changes must serve as an implicit meter — legible through observation, not UI.
Anti-Pattern: Visible MeterOptimization vs. Authenticity
Cross-Game Synthesis

The most emotionally impactful multi-ending games share one property: the ending makes prior behavior retroactively visible. The player does not feel scored — they feel seen. For KENOMA, this means the Vanguard's Child Phase behavior must function as a mirror of the Scholar's entire Camel Phase arc. What she has become, how she moves through the room, whether she hesitates — all of it must feel like a reading of who the Scholar was, not a reward/punishment binary.

Scoring Philosophy

The score distribution encodes a clear character argument: scholars who read deeply, notice details, share resources, and remain present in the Archive trend toward Colorful. Scholars who hoard, follow self-preservation instincts, and disengage trend toward Poor. Neither is wrong — the spectrum is a reflection system, not a judgment system. But the game has a thesis, and the thesis produces a scoring gradient.

06 · Affective Design

Beyond Scoring: What Makes Players Feel the Endings

A scoring system only produces emotions if the player's behavior is made visible to them at the right moment. The following analysis examines the specific mechanisms through which KENOMA's endings can transcend their numerical origins and land as genuine emotional experiences.

Mechanism How It Works in KENOMA Target Emotion Risk if Fails
Retroactive Revelation The Vanguard's Child Phase behavior recasts every prior Camel Phase scene. The oil flask that seemed like generosity now reads as data collection. The Stage 3 moment where she completed a puzzle independently, which felt like pride, now reads as capability assessment. The endings do not add new information — they reframe existing information. Recognition, complicity Endings feel arbitrary if Camel Phase didn't seed enough material to reframe
The Mirror Test Each ending's specific behaviors must feel like a reading of this player's specific Scholar, not a generic Vanguard mode. The Digital Twin stopping one inch from the player's own unset trap is only devastating if that trap was placed during gameplay. The behavioral model is only a mirror if it reflects actual behavior. Intimate horror of being known Endings feel generic if VanguardBehaviorModel doesn't reference actual player data
Observatory as Physical Score The Child Phase arena is the Observatory — its corruption state is the visible, physical expression of the behavioral score. A scholar with Lv.1 scores enters a heavily corrupted room; a Lv.5 scholar enters a space that still feels like theirs. The ending's starting conditions encode the ending's meaning before a word is spoken or a blow struck. Setting dread / earned dignity Physical score legibility fails if corruption states aren't visually distinct enough
The Compass Silence The compass goes silent at the Child Phase — for the first time in the entire game. Six to nine hours of continuous static warning, then nothing. The silence communicates more than any UI element could: there is nothing left to warn against. The Scholar is past warning. This affective payload requires the compass to have been consistently present throughout play. Finality, inevitability Falls flat if players tuned out compass static as audio clutter
The False Victory Architecture The 25-second False Victory sequence conditions the player to relax — achievement pop, breathing normalization, save prompt — before the tear opens. The emotional impact of the ending is amplified by how completely the player believed, for those 25 seconds, that they had won. This works only if the T+6s save prompt is accepted; many players will save, recording their own relief in the moment before the ending begins. Betrayal of relaxation, retrospective dread Fails completely if players expect the tear. Cannot be spoiled.
The Fan Li Inversion The opening cinematic has the Scholar quote Fan Li's aphorism as proof that they understand it — and immediately misapply it. "He meant it as a warning. I am not a bow." By Lv.4–5 endings, the player understands that the Scholar was the bow, spoke the aphorism that named their own fate, and misread it. The ending lands hardest on second playthrough when the player hears the opening VO with full retrospective knowledge. Tragic irony, self-recognition Requires players to remember the opening VO; auditory callback needs to be placed in Child Phase
Vanguard's Three-Second Silence (Child Phase Scenarios per GDD v4 KENOMA Core) In the five Child Phase scenarios described in the KENOMA Core skill, the Vanguard moves through rooms the Scholar worked in, with a three-second silence appearing in every scenario. Its weight changes with each scenario — administrative in Scenario I, prayerlike in Scenario V. This structural gesture must be present in all ten endings as an affective anchor: the Vanguard standing still in the Scholar's last room is the emotional center of every resolution. Grief, completion, dignity Fails if the silence is unearned — requires the room to carry visible evidence of Scholar activity
The Core Affective Requirement

None of the above mechanisms work if the player does not have a genuine relationship with the Vanguard before the Child Phase. The Camel Phase must build attachment — to the Archive, to the Vanguard, to the Scholar's work — that the Lion Phase strains and the Child Phase resolves. An ending that is scored correctly but built on an unattached Camel Phase will land as spectacle, not tragedy. The affective architecture is load-bearing.

07 · Trade-off Analysis

Design Costs at Each Level of the Spectrum

🔻
Lv.1–2 (Poor Range)
The Cost of Being Punishing
These endings serve the game's thesis (absent engagement produces diminished results) but risk feeling like punishment for casual players. If a first-time player reaches The Shell-Shocked Conscript, they may attribute the outcome to difficulty rather than behavioral failure — missing the game's point entirely. The "ugliest outcome for both characters" framing must be careful: ugly can read as the game being unkind, not as the game being honest.
TRADE-OFF: Thesis fidelity vs. first-playthrough reception
⚖️
Lv.3 (Neutral)
The Cost of Genre Satisfaction
The Eastern Zhou Strategist is tonally the most "correct" ending — efficient, precise, emotionless. But it risks reading as genre-satisfying in a way that blunts the tragedy. Horror players who have been seeking the competent villain will feel vindicated rather than implicated. Lv.3 is also the most likely outcome for a normal playthrough, which means it is the ending most players will carry away. Its emotional payload must be carefully calibrated.
TRADE-OFF: Genre coherence vs. emotional impact depth
🔑
Lv.4–5 (Colorful)
The Cost of Requiring Depth
The Digital Twin and The Architect of Clean Architecture are the most emotionally complete endings — but they require a level of engagement that most first-playthrough players will not achieve. GDD targets: no more than 15% of first-run players reach Colorful endings. This means the game's best content is structurally inaccessible on first play. This is philosophically correct (the endings reward the kind of scholarly engagement the Scholar embodies) but commercially risky.
TRADE-OFF: Earned reward vs. content accessibility
🪞
Invisible Tracking
The Cost of No Feedback
The BMS invisibility is the game's most philosophically important architectural decision. It is also the system most likely to produce player frustration on endings that feel arbitrary. Players who don't notice the Vanguard's behavioral changes (the only implicit feedback channel) will have no way to course-correct. The design commits to this as a feature: the game is not teaching the player to optimize, it is making the player's character visible to them. But "feature" and "frustration" coexist in this choice.
TRADE-OFF: Authenticity vs. legibility
🔀
Independent World Scores
The Cost of Asymmetric Outcomes
WA 85 / WB 15 produces a radically different experience than WA 50 / WB 50, even though both represent "similar total engagement." The split-score system is philosophically rich (specialists exist; the Scholar who understood the material world but not the spiritual one is a real character) but complicates the narrative coherence of any given run. Both Vanguards must feel like plausible outcomes of the same Scholar. Lv.5 WA with Lv.1 WB creates a Scholar who gave everything to one axis — is that legible as a character?
TRADE-OFF: Specificity vs. narrative coherence
⏳
False Victory Timing
The Highest-Variance Risk
The 25-second False Victory sequence is the most precisely-tuned element in the entire game. Too short: players don't relax. Too long: players become suspicious. The save prompt at T+6s is the fulcrum — players who save are recording their own relief, making the subsequent devastation more personal. But some players will not save, and some will immediately reload the save if the next content feels like it "broke" the game. The sequence cannot be patched post-launch without fundamentally altering the emotional architecture.
TRADE-OFF: Peak emotional impact vs. irreducible variance
08 · Risk Register

What Could Break the Spectrum

HIGH
BMS events not surfacing through behavior. The most catastrophic failure mode: the scoring is running correctly, but the Vanguard's behavioral changes are too subtle for players to register. If playtesters describe only their own actions ("I chose path X") and not the Vanguard's response, the feedback loop is broken. The ending will feel assigned, not earned.
→ demo-check gate
HIGH
Lv.1 endings read as punishment, not reflection. Players who receive Shell-Shocked Conscript or Drowning Victim may attribute the outcome to "the game was too hard" rather than "I was not present enough." The framing must make the Scholar's absence the subject — not the player's skill failure.
→ narrative-guardian review
HIGH
False Victory spoiled. The entire affective architecture of the Child Phase depends on the T+9s tear arriving as a surprise. A single prominent review, playthrough video, or social media spoiler eliminates this for every player who encounters it afterward. The sequence has no fallback once spoiled.
→ embargo + press briefing
MED
Score distribution produces inaccessible spectrum bands. If the natural play of a careful first-run player produces Lv.2-3 outcomes regardless of engagement depth, the Colorful endings become effectively inaccessible without external guidance — undermining the replay promise. QA must verify all five bands are reachable via deliberate play.
→ QA spectrum validation
MED
Dark Voice Follow path scores too negative. If following the Dark Voice produces a Lv.1 ending for players who otherwise engaged deeply, the sabotage branch feels punitive rather than philosophically coherent. The −4/session penalty needs to be tuned against the actual depth of engagement in a typical Follow playthrough.
→ playtest branch analysis
MED
Asymmetric world scores break narrative coherence. A Scholar with WA 85 / WB 15 needs both a credible WA ending and a credible WB ending — but they represent opposite character portraits of the same Scholar. The Lv.5 WA / Lv.1 WB combination must be playtested specifically to confirm both endpoints feel like possible descriptions of the same person.
→ narrative-guardian review
LOW
Health hoarding penalty invisible to players. The −3/checkpoint for high health maintenance is the most subtle negative modifier in the system. Players who are simply cautious (not disengaged) may be penalized without understanding why. The signal must be legible through Vanguard behavior: a scholar who never takes damage should produce a slightly colder Vanguard in the Camel Phase.
→ bms-architect tuning
LOW
Child Phase BMS scenarios vs. GDD 10-scenario spec misalignment. The KENOMA Core skill describes five Child Phase scenarios (Emptiness through Transcendence) using a different taxonomy than the GDD's 10-scenario ending spectrum. Both reference different structural elements. These must be reconciled before content production begins — the five scenarios and the ten endings may represent different systems operating in sequence.
→ narrative-guardian alignment

The KENOMA Core skill's five Child Phase scenarios (Emptiness → Grief → Respect → Completion → Transcendence) appear to describe what the surviving Vanguard does after the Scholar is gone — an epilogue sequence. The GDD v5's ten endings describe the Child Phase arena encounter itself. These may be complementary systems: the arena encounter (one of ten) followed by the epilogue (one of five). If so, the epilogue scenario may be driven by a combined score of both world registers, producing a separate resolution layer. This needs explicit architectural confirmation.

09 · Recommendations

What the Ending Architecture Needs to Succeed

01
Reconcile the Two Ending Taxonomies
The GDD v5 and KENOMA Core skill describe different ending systems. Before content production, establish definitively: are the five epilogue scenarios a separate system from the ten arena encounters? If yes, does the epilogue scenario selection use both world scores combined, or just the surviving world's score? This architectural question determines how much ending content needs to be produced.
Suggested architecture: 10 arena encounters (WA×5 or WB×5) + 5 epilogue scenarios = up to 50 combinations, but with most combinations using proximity scoring. Document the combination matrix.
02
Add Implicit Score Feedback Through Vanguard Stage Changes
The score is invisible but the Vanguard's stage transitions are not — they just need to be detectable. The transition from S2 to S3 (she begins anticipating rather than asking) is the game's primary implicit score indicator. This moment must be unmissable in playtesting. If players don't notice it, they have no feedback channel. Consider adding a camera composition that subtly highlights her behavioral shift at the key transition.
The moment she completes a puzzle subset independently while the Scholar works on the main sequence (Act I beat, late Camel) is the correct signal delivery point. This scene should be designed as the game's most carefully composed camera moment.
03
Design Lv.1–2 Endings for Recognition, Not Punishment
The Shell-Shocked Conscript and Drowning Victim are emotionally devastating in the right direction only if players understand that what they produced is a consequence of their absence, not the game being unfair. The framing must foreground the Vanguard's degradation as loss, not threat. She should look diminished in a way that produces pity — and that pity should redirect toward the Scholar. "What did I not give her?"

04
Build the Fan Li Callback Into Every Ending
The opening VO has the Scholar misquote Fan Li's warning. Every ending should contain one auditory or visual callback to that misreading — not as an explicit callback ("you were wrong"), but as the correct interpretation made visible. The bow being put away. The hunting dog cooked. The specific image from the aphorism should be physically present in the arena in some form appropriate to the ending's register.
Lv.5 WA: The Architect physically picks up the Scholar's spring-pistol before the final strike. The bow is put away — in her hands. Lv.5 WB: The Author writes the final passage. The text of the aphorism appears, completed, in the closing image.
05
Treat the VanguardBehaviorModel as the True Ending Generator
The most emotionally impactful aspect of any ending is not the scripted sequence — it is the Vanguard's use of the player's actual behavioral data. The Digital Twin stopping one inch from the player's own unset trap is only devastating if the trap was actually there. Invest in the VanguardBehaviorModel referencing real session data in the Child Phase. Scripted behavior plus behavioral model creates endings that feel authored and personal simultaneously.
Each ending script should have [BEHAVIOR_MODEL_INSERT] tags at specific moments — points where the Vanguard references a specific observed behavior from the player's actual run. These inserts must be designed in advance as a library of "observed" behaviors that the scripted sequences can accommodate.
06
Make the Compass Silence a Designed Experience, Not a Consequence
The compass going silent at the Child Phase is listed as a consequence of the Guardian having no function. It should be designed as a deliberate, composed moment: the Scholar notices the silence before the tear opens. The game pauses for a beat — long enough for the player to register that something is missing. This silence should feel like a bell that has stopped, not a system that turned off. It should precede the tear, not coincide with it.
Suggested timing: compass silence at T+24s (per current spec). Add Scholar animation: they reach for the compass, look at it. It is still. They put it away. This takes 3 seconds. The tear opens at T+9s before this — the silence confirms what the tear began.

§ 00 · The Game's DNAFive Core Ideas — Everything Flows From These
If a document, mechanic, or dialogue line doesn't serve one of these, it shouldn't exist
01 The Tragedy of Competence
The Scholar is exactly good enough to be used completely. Not despite their brilliance — because of it. They translate what only they can translate, cross what only they can cross, and build the thing that will outlive them with genuine joy. The tragedy is that they were exactly right for this. The Archive needed them. They needed the Archive. Both things were true.
Guard against: Making the Scholar feel passive or victimized. They chose this. Every single day, they chose it.
02 Warmth and Calculation Are the Same Thing
The Vanguard places an exact measure of oil in the Scholar's hands — not one drop more than their demonstrated consumption rate requires. This is a calculation. It is also an act of care. Both are completely true in the same gesture. The game does not resolve this. It holds both. The oil flask is the game.
Guard against: Resolving the warmth into either pure manipulation or pure sentiment. She genuinely cares. She also always knew how it would end.
03 Understanding Is the Weapon
The Vanguard is not tracking the Scholar to eliminate them. She is tracking them to understand them. Elimination is the consequence of understanding, not the goal. The more the Scholar invests, the more completely they are understood. A complete model is a Vanguard who knows exactly when the work is done. The Scholar who gives everything builds the most formidable ending.
Guard against: The BMS becoming visible. The player must read the model through the Vanguard's behavior — never through a UI element.
04 The Scholar Is the Only Bridge
The Archive was built for three. The two Vanguards have everything except the ability to cross. The Scholar has everything except the ability to stay. The three-body system is the building's architecture — baked into Clause 18, 300 years before the Scholar arrived. Nietzsche's rope over the abyss. The bridge is great because it is crossed, not because it survives.
Guard against: The Iron Rule ever bending. Knowledge from World B enables action in World A. Material from World A changes World B physically. One rule. Everywhere. Always.
The Ending Is Not a Twist. It Is a Completion.
"You were extraordinary. Past tense is not a judgment. It is a precision." — Five scenario archetypes. Each one a different story about what these two people were to each other. Level 5 is not the good ending. It is the ending where both became fully what they were — at the same moment — through each other. A player who reaches Level 5 built the thing that eliminated them. No game has done this before. This is why KENOMA exists.
§ 01 · The ExperienceWhat Each Act Must Feel Like — Before Any System Is Built
Story first. If a mechanic doesn't produce this feeling, the mechanic is wrong
Act I · The Camel Professional Delight Shading Into Dread
The Archive is the finest library the Scholar has ever entered. Every puzzle is the correct use of a life's training. The Vanguard is efficient and strange and familiar in a way the Scholar cannot place. Both things feel like gifts. The player is warm and comfortable and belonging here — and the dread enters so quietly they don't notice it arriving. They only notice it when it's already been there for twenty minutes. The warmth must be genuine before the turn can devastate.
"I had a translation. Yours is better." — She means it. She also chose that text deliberately. Both things are true.
Act II · The Lion Urgent, Intelligent, Slightly Sad
The Scholar is very good at this too. Environmental chess using everything the Camel Phase built. The Dark Voice stops surprising them — they've started agreeing before it finishes speaking. This is not a horror chase. This is two brilliant people in the same building, both doing everything correctly, one trying to finish before the other finishes first. The sadness is that neither of them is wrong.
The Scholar sets down the tablet. Picks up the crossbow. The game does not cut away.
Act III · The Child Aftermath — A Different Kind of Silence
The player watches rather than acts. The Vanguard moves through rooms the Scholar worked in, reading the residue of who they were. The three-second silence is the same gesture in every scenario — only its weight changes. In Scenario I it feels like emptiness. In Scenario V it feels like prayer. The Archive registers completion. She straightens her tunic. She goes north. The fire altars stay lit behind her. This is the Nietzschean Child: not innocent, but new.
"The birds are gone now." — Level 5, World B. The Fan Li aphorism, completed. By her, not the Scholar.

§ 04 · The PathSeven Creative Decisions — From First Read to Playable Story
Not a sprint plan. A sequence of commitments, each one unlocking the next.
Before Any of This The whole team reads three things aloud together. First: the Scholar's Day Three journal entry — they turn the page on Fan Li's warning and start the creature taxonomy. Second: the oil flask scene (mid-Camel, WA-002 corridor). Third: the Clause 18 declaration — Vanguard A to the Scholar, Stage 4+. If the room goes quiet after the Clause 18 scene, the team is ready to build. If it doesn't, more time with the material before production starts.

Weeks 1–2 · Everyone · No tools yet Know the Characters Well Enough to Defend Them
The Vanguard taught herself Babylonian for 8 years so she could understand her Scholar when they arrived. The Scholar wrote Fan Li's warning in the margin of their journal and turned the page. Every team member must understand these two people well enough to know when a proposed dialogue line, mechanic, or room behavior contradicts who they are. This takes longer than it seems and is worth every minute.

Weeks 2–3 · Engineering + Design Build the Behavioral Model System First — Everything Writes to It
The BMS is the invisible connective tissue of the game. 12 core event types. Batched per room-session, not per frame. WarmthRegister per Vanguard. Weeks 3–6 · Art + Audio + Design Make the Deep Read Feel Like the Best Moment in the Game
The Scholar reading a tablet deeply — 25 to 40 seconds — is KENOMA's primary player action. Everything downstream depends on players choosing to do it. The Scholar's voice murmuring the translation. Cuneiform characters illuminating as they're parsed. The ambient world narrowing to focus around one warm light. If this interaction feels slow or unrewarding, players skim. If they skim, the BMS receives shallow data, the Vanguard's recognition stages stall, the endings collapse into adjacent indistinctions. Art and audio must prioritize this above everything else in the vertical slice.

§ 05 · ConstraintsWhat KENOMA Must Never Do — The Lines That Protect the Core
Four rules. Break any one and the game becomes something that already exists.
Never Make the Turn a Betrayal
The Vanguard was always this person. The Act I→II turn is not a reveal. It is a completion. If any team member describes it as a betrayal, reread the Clause 18 scene. VA looks at the Scholar and says, in the clearest possible terms, exactly what is about to happen — and then walks away to execute it. She told them. She has always told them. The Scholar heard it and stayed anyway. The turn is the final gift of the Camel Phase.
If playtesters say "I didn't see that coming" — the Camel Phase failed. If they say "I knew and it still landed" — the game worked.
Never Break the Iron Rule
Knowledge from World B enables action in World A. Material from World A produces physical change in World B. One rule. Taught in PZ-002. Consistent everywhere. TS2 had six different crossing logics across seven zones — it destroyed player intuition completely. KENOMA has one. If a puzzle design requires bending the rule, the puzzle design changes. The rule does not.
Not even once, not even with good intentions. The moment the rule bends, every subsequent crossing becomes uncertain.
Never Show the Behavioral Model
No dialogue wheels. No "your choice has been remembered." No score counter. No UI element of any kind that references the BMS. The player reads the behavioral model through one channel only: the Vanguard's observed behavior changes. Stage 5 is the most frightening moment in the game — it must arrive through a player noticing that she has stopped asking questions she used to ask. Never through a notification.
The moment the tracking is visible, every prior warmth retroactively becomes a checkbox. The relationship collapses.
Never Make a Puzzle That Doesn't Have an Author
Every puzzle in the Archive exists because a real person made a real decision in that space. "This room needs a puzzle" is never a valid reason. The question is always: who was here, what did they need, what did they leave? The Grand Armillary's activation sequence is in the manual in WA-003 because the people who built it feared what they had created and included an emergency dissolution protocol. That is why the puzzle is hard — because it was designed for an emergency.
If a puzzle can't be explained by naming the person who designed it and why, it doesn't belong in the Archive.

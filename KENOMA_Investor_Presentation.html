02 · The Product — In Plain Terms
What KENOMA Actually Is
Without any gaming background required. The concept explains itself in one paragraph — and that concept is why it matters.
The Concept — One Paragraph You play a scholar investigating a sealed Persian archive. During the first half of the game, you work alongside a companion — a general who has been alone in this place for 300 years. You teach her things. You share knowledge. You give her tools. She seems grateful. She genuinely cares about you. In the second half, she uses everything you gave her to eliminate you. How well she does this — how sophisticated her strategy is, how devastating her execution — depends entirely on what you invested in her. Players who gave her everything meet the most formidable version of her. Players who held back meet a lesser one. The game ends in a spectrum of 10 possible outcomes, ranging from a kind of mercy to a kind of perfection. None of them are "winning." All of them are earned.

Why This Concept Has Commercial Legs

The most successful narrative games share one property: they create a moment that players cannot stop talking about. The ending of The Last of Us. The twist in Bioshock. The true ending of Undertale. These moments generate enormous organic marketing — players tell other people, who buy the game specifically to experience the moment.

KENOMA's mechanic is designed to produce exactly this kind of moment — but with a crucial difference: the moment is personalized. Every player builds a slightly different version of the adversary, which means every player has a slightly different story to tell. Social media, content creators, and word-of-mouth work disproportionately hard for this type of experience.

Additionally: the 10-ending spectrum produces replay value that most story games lack. Players return to build the adversary differently. Each playthrough is a different experience of the same 4-hour structure.

The IP — What Is Actually Being Built

The game is the delivery vehicle. The asset is the intellectual property: specifically, the "protagonist-built antagonist" mechanic — a system where the player's investments throughout the game construct the quality of the final adversary. No product in the industry has shipped this mechanic. It is protectable, licensable, and applicable beyond a single game.

The Persian setting — the Archive, the two worlds, the dual civilization story — creates a franchise architecture. The first game (Chapter 1: The Scholar) establishes the world. The IP can support sequels in the same setting, adaptations in different settings using the same core mechanic, and licensing to other studios.

The comparables — Disco Elysium, Balatro — prove that novel mechanic + strong world = franchise IP that grows beyond its first product.

05 · Flexibility
What Can Change Without Losing the Core
Smart investment requires understanding what is fixed and what is adaptable. Here is the honest breakdown.

KENOMA has a core that cannot change without losing what makes it valuable — and a surface that can adapt significantly to market conditions, audience feedback, or investor priorities. Knowing the difference between the two is the most important thing an investor can understand about this product.

Must Keep — These Are the IP The Core That Cannot Move
🔒The protagonist-built antagonist mechanic. This is the product's unique value. Remove it and you have a standard horror game competing with hundreds of others. This is what investors are paying for.
🔒The companion-to-adversary arc. The player must genuinely like the companion before the turn. This emotional investment is what makes the ending meaningful. Without it, the mechanic is just a difficulty slider.
🔒Invisible behavioral tracking. The player must not know they are being evaluated. Announced choice points (like most games) produce strategic behavior. Invisible tracking produces authentic behavior — and authentic behavior produces the personalized ending the mechanic promises.
🔒Granular outcome spectrum. The 10 endings (simplified to 3 in Phase 1) are what creates replay value and social media discussion. Binary good/bad endings are too common to generate the conversation that drives organic marketing.
Can Change — These Are Adaptable The Surface That Can Flex
🔄The setting. The Persian archive is the current setting — historically underrepresented in games, which is a differentiator. But the core mechanic works in any world: Japanese feudal + spirit world, Victorian mansion + underground, Egyptian living + dead. If market research suggests a different setting resonates better, the setting changes. The mechanic does not.
🔄The horror intensity. Currently positioned as psychological horror. Can be repositioned as "psychological mystery" or "narrative thriller" to reach a broader audience. The core concept does not require horror elements — it requires an emotionally invested companion and a consequential ending.
🔄The scope of Chapter 1. 3–4 hours is the current target. Could be 2 hours at a lower price point ($9.99) to reduce friction and maximize trial, then upsell to Chapter 2. Or 6 hours at $25 for a more complete first experience. This is a market positioning decision, not a design decision.
🔄The medium itself. The "protagonist-built antagonist" mechanic is not exclusive to games. It translates to interactive film (Netflix format), graphic novel with reader-tracked reading behavior, or a tabletop experience. The IP is the mechanic. The delivery format is a business decision.

The Adaptation Scenarios — If Market Conditions Shift

Scenario A · Broaden the Audience Remove the horror classification. Reposition as "psychological mystery drama." Same core mechanic. Same companion arc. Different marketing language. This opens the product to players who don't identify as horror fans — roughly 3× the addressable audience. Comparable: What Remains of Edith Finch reached non-gaming audiences by avoiding genre classification.
Scenario B · Partner with a Publisher Phase 1's commercial success makes KENOMA an attractive acquisition for mid-size publishers (Devolver Digital, Annapurna Interactive, Raw Fury) who specialize in narrative games with strong IP potential. A publisher deal funds Phase 2 in exchange for a revenue share — reducing investor risk while preserving upside. This is the most common path for successful indie Phase 1 products.
Scenario C · The IP Play If Phase 1 generates significant critical attention but modest commercial numbers — common for genuinely innovative games in their first release — the IP is positioned for a strategic sale or licensing deal. The "protagonist-built antagonist" mechanic as licensed technology has value independent of any specific game. This is the downside protection.

Risk Assessment — Honest

Risk · Discovery Visibility on Steam is hard
778 indie horror games were released on Steam in 2024. Only 7.2% were "successful." Mitigation: Chapter 1's episodic model allows for targeted pre-launch community building. The mechanic's novelty is a natural PR story — game journalists actively cover "unprecedented mechanics." Budget includes $30–40K specifically for visibility.
Risk · Execution The mechanic must feel earned, not manipulative
If players feel deceived rather than moved, the word-of-mouth turns negative. Mitigation: This risk is entirely a design and writing quality question — not a budget question. The vertical slice demo exists specifically to test this before committing to full production.
Protection · Downside The IP retains value independent of commercial performance
A well-received but commercially modest Chapter 1 is an acquisition target. Publishers like Annapurna Interactive and Devolver Digital acquire narrative game studios based on critical reception and IP potential, not just sales figures. The downside scenario is not "the investment is lost" — it is "the IP sells at a price that recovers a portion of the investment."
Protection · Upside Horror games overperform on content platforms
Horror is the #1 genre for YouTube "let's play" content and Twitch streaming. A single popular content creator playing Chapter 1 can generate 50,000–500,000 views — equivalent to a significant marketing budget at zero cost. Disco Elysium, Signalis, and Dredge all attribute a substantial portion of their sales to content creator coverage they did not pay for.

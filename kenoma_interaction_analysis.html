How Horror Games Tell You

Game Analysis

Alone in the Dark (1992)
Infogrames · DOS · the original
1992

Interaction is mediated through an action mode menu opened by the Enter key. The player selects a verb — Fight, Search/Open, Close, Push, Jump (late-game only) — then presses Spacebar while positioned close to the target. No highlight. No indicator. No prompt of any kind.

The player discovers interactable objects through proximity trial-and-error and prior world knowledge (is this desk the kind of thing someone would hide something in?). Objects picked up by walking over them. The weight-based inventory gives a secondary signal: if picking something up is refused, the inventory is full, not that the object is non-interactive.

The radical consequence: the camera is the indicator. Every fixed camera angle in AitD is authored to show the relevant interactive object in frame. The room designer and the camera designer are the same intention — the player is always looking at what they need to find. The system trusts compositional cinema to do the job UI would normally do.

Alone in the Dark 3 (1995)
Infogrames · DOS · same engine, wider world
1995

The same engine and action-menu system as AitD 1 — Fight, Open/Examine, Push, Jump — with Spacebar as the universal action trigger. No changes to the interaction model. The open-world ghost town (Slaughter Gulch) spreads interactable objects across a larger space, making the trial-and-error discovery system more taxing than in the confined manor of AitD 1.

AitD 3 introduces a structural refinement the interaction system did not need: more of the game is action-combat-gated than puzzle-gated. Two invincible gunslingers block most paths, forcing a specific exploration order. The "Search" action therefore encounters objects that are contextually useful only at a specific time — the player is more often searching a room they cannot yet solve than in AitD 1.

The search mechanic's cognitive load increases when the space is larger but the indicator system is identical. A game that trusts composition to signal importance needs tight, authored spaces. Slaughter Gulch reveals this limit.

Alone in the Dark: The New Nightmare (2001)
Darkworks · PS1/DC/PC · the flashlight pivot
2001

The interaction paradigm shifts from menu-verb to light-as-query. Carnby and Aline each carry a flashlight controlled independently via the right analogue stick. When the beam hits an interactable surface or item, the object visually reveals itself — materials catch and reflect the light differently. The player scans the environment with the flashlight; the environment responds to where they look.

This is a genuine design breakthrough. The flashlight serves three simultaneous functions: navigation tool, combat weapon (light damages shadow creatures and holds plant-enemies at bay), and interaction indicator. Every beam movement is gameplay. The indicator cost is zero because the player is already using the light for survival.

The execution has a known failure: exact positioning is still required to trigger interactions, even after the flashlight reveals them. The reveal moment and the pickup moment are decoupled — sometimes significantly. A player who sees the object in their beam must then manoeuvre to a specific 1×1m interaction zone. The gap between "I can see it" and "I can reach it" is the system's critical friction point.

Silent Hill 1 & 2 (1999 / 2001)
Team Silent · PS1/PS2 · the head-turn paradigm
1999–2001

Silent Hill 1 uses a third-person free camera — interactable objects are visually distinguishable but the system relies on proximity and a contextual "Check" prompt that appears on screen when close enough. The radio static system (louder = closer creature) is a separate channel but establishes the principle: the character's body communicates what the UI does not.

Silent Hill 2 refines this into the head-turn mechanic. James Sunderland's head rotates toward interactable objects when he approaches them — automatically, diegetically, without any UI element. The player following James's gaze finds the interactable. Two forces make this work: (1) tank controls require the player to monitor the character's facing direction constantly; (2) people instinctively look where other people look. The filmmaking principle of eyeline-match is transplanted into real-time 3D navigation.

No other game in the genre has matched the elegance of this system for fixed-character-facing control schemes. It is free. No UI, no prompt, no indicator layer — only an animation state the player must have been watching anyway to navigate.

Resident Evil (1996–2002, REmake)
Capcom · pre-rendered + 3D · two-layer system
1996–2002

Pre-rendered RE uses a two-layer system: artistic and systemic. The artistic layer: items are visually distinct within the pre-rendered background — brighter, slightly out-of-place, or compositionally centred. The background artist signals importance through visual design. The systemic layer: a context-sensitive "Check" or "Examine" verb appears on screen when the player enters the interaction radius of an object.

The REmake (2002) codifies this: yellow-highlighted surfaces for graspable environmental elements (ledges, windowsills), and a proximity prompt (▲ Examine) for collectibles. The yellow paint approach trades atmosphere for clarity. It is the first appearance of what would become the dominant modern convention — coloured surface marking as affordance indicator.

The examine system, present since RE1, allows objects in inventory to be rotated in 3D to find hidden items (keys inside books, jewels inside statuettes). This is a rare moment where examination itself is the mechanic, not merely the preamble to pickup.

Tormented Souls (2021)
Dual Effect · modern fixed-cam · cursor highlight + FP examine
2021

Tormented Souls takes a pragmatic modern approach: an on-screen cursor highlights interactable points of interest as the player approaches. The highlight is subtle — not a glowing outline, closer to a visual callout — but it is definitively non-diegetic. The cursor is a concession to discoverability.

The system's genuine innovation is the first-person perspective shift on interaction. When examining an object, the camera cuts to a first-person close-up view. The player can rotate items, examine surfaces in detail, and combine items from the same screen without menu-switching. The inventory is also accessible directly from this view. The moment of examination is immersive even though the indicator leading up to it is not.

This split — non-diegetic to find, diegetic to examine — is honest about the genre's fundamental tension: classic horror games are better atmospherically, but worse at showing players where to look. Tormented Souls picks both, at the cost of a slight tonal inconsistency.

Amnesia / SOMA / Frictional Games (2010–2015)
Frictional · first-person physics · the "just try it" doctrine
2010–2015

Frictional's interaction philosophy is physics-first, indicator-never. In Amnesia and SOMA, no highlight, no prompt, no verb menu. The player clicks and drags objects directly — doors pulled open by dragging backward, drawers pulled by clicking and pulling, lids lifted by arc-motion. The spatial logic of the real world is the affordance. If it looks like something you could grab, grab it.

The cursor changes to a hand icon when hovering over an interactable — a minimal concession. Otherwise: the game trusts the player to try things. The cost is that unprepared players miss significant objects entirely. The benefit is that a player discovering a working drawer by pulling it feels like discovering a real drawer. The action is the same. No abstraction between intent and execution.

This system only works in first-person, free-mouse environments. Fixed cameras make physics-drag interaction geometrically unreliable — the screen-space drag direction does not map to a consistent world-space direction as cameras cut.

Metro 2033 / Last Light / Exodus (2010–2019)
4A Games · total physicalization · the wristwatch model
2010–2019

Metro's interaction philosophy is total physicalization of all status information — the system relevant to KENOMA. Every game-state indicator lives in a physical object the character carries: wristwatch (timer, radiation count), backpack (open physically to access map), lighter fluid gauge (visible on lighter), gas mask filters (visually degrade, physically changed). The world is the HUD.

Interactable objects are discovered through environmental design priority: important items are placed in visually prominent positions — on desks, at eye level, lit differently by ambient sources. The game never highlights them explicitly. In Exodus, the backpack-opening animation (character physically reaches behind and brings out the pack) is a masterclass in diegetic UI design.

The failure mode: discoverability depends entirely on level design quality. When an object is placed poorly — in a dark corner, below natural sightlines — players miss it with no recourse. Metro solves this with tight level design; a game with wider, more open spaces would need a supplementary layer.

Adaptive Designs for KENOMA

Five designs for KENOMA's specific constraints: fixed cameras, tank controls, zero HUD (Iron Law III), jade lamp as primary light source, astrolabe as creature-proximity instrument, Scholar as the only mobile intelligence in the Archive.

01
Scholar's Gaze Architecture
The SH2 head-turn — adapted for fixed camera realities
ORIGIN · Silent Hill 2
TIER · Level 2A

The Scholar's head and upper body orient toward an interactable object when entering a defined proximity radius. The rotation is subtle — not a snap, a slow draw of attention. The player, navigating with tank controls, is already watching the Scholar's facing direction to navigate; the head-turn communicates without cost.

Fixed-camera adaptation: The primary failure mode of the head-turn in fixed-camera games is that certain camera angles show only the Scholar's back, making head direction unreadable. The solution: KENOMA's camera authoring rules (every camera frames Scholar + Vanguard + key object simultaneously) must also guarantee the Scholar's face is readable in the frame when near a critical interactive object. The camera selection algorithm includes a boolean: does this angle make the Scholar's head direction legible? If not, the camera transitions to an angle that does, at the same moment the head-turn fires.

For objects the Scholar approaches from behind (inscriptions on far walls, sealed drawers), the body-lean system supplements head-turn: the Scholar's torso inclines forward at a rate proportional to distance. This is readable from any camera angle that shows the Scholar's silhouette.

The posture signal also differentiates object types: head-turn for text/tablet interactions, body-lean for physical/mechanism interactions, hands-extended for inventory-use interactions. Three distinct postures, each communicating the category of interaction before the player commits.

02
Fire Vessel Sympathy
The jade lamp responds to interactable resonance — AitD:TNN adapted for an always-carried light source
ORIGIN · AitD: New Nightmare
TIER · Level 2B

The Scholar's jade lamp — already the primary status display (flame height = oil level) and a combat tool — develops a third behavior: the flame brightens and orients directionally toward interactable objects in proximity, like a compass needle responding to magnetic north.

This is diegetic within KENOMA's established world logic: the Archive's objects are resonant with the flame of the Sacred Fire (World A) or receptive to warmth after centuries of cold (World B). The flame responding to an inscribed tablet or a sealed mechanism is a world-logic extension, not an arbitrary UI choice.

The orientation trick: Rather than brightening the whole flame uniformly, the flame leans toward the interactable — the direction of lean tells the player where to look. In World B, where the fire vessel is the Scholar's only warmth anchor and everything else is cold, the lean becomes particularly readable: the flame reaching toward a tablet in darkness is a visible event even in low-light conditions.

Critical design constraint: the sympathy response must be clearly distinct from the oil-depletion response. Depletion = flame shrinks centrally and sputters. Sympathy = flame maintains size, shifts directionally, slightly brightens at the edge facing the object. These are visually distinct behaviours but require careful VFX authoring to ensure they remain unambiguous under pressure.

The system only works for objects with physical presence and Archive significance. It does not fire for enemy proximity (astrolabe handles that), it does not fire for mundane furniture. The Scholar's fire responds to the Archive — a reading-intelligence distinction, not a proximity-sphere.

03
Astrolabe Resonance Differentiation
The creature-proximity instrument gains a second vibration mode for interactable objects
ORIGIN · SH1 Radio Static (logic) · AitD (astrolabe)
TIER · Level 3A

The Scholar's astrolabe already vibrates at increasing frequency as creatures approach — a direct analogue to Silent Hill's radio static. The design question: can a single instrument communicate two distinct types of proximity without creating confusion?

The answer is frequency and quality rather than volume. Creature proximity: the astrolabe rings emit a rapid, irregular, slightly discordant vibration — the sound of something living and wrong approaching. Interactable object proximity: a slow, even, harmonic vibration — the sound of something old and precise awaiting recognition.

The distinction is the same one the game makes conceptually: creatures are threats; Archive objects are knowledge. The scholar's instrument responds to both, but differently in character. A player who has learned the creature signal will feel the interactable signal as its tonal opposite — not alarming, but attentive.

The system combines with the flame sympathy (Design 02) most effectively: the astrolabe tells the Scholar something significant is nearby; the flame's directional lean tells them where. Together they provide proximity detection and direction without any UI element, and without requiring the player to be looking at the Scholar at the specific moment of detection.

Critical failure mode: dual-signal confusion in rooms containing both creatures and interactable objects simultaneously. In these rooms, the astrolabe must produce both signals layered rather than blended — the creature signal running over the interactable signal as a separate frequency layer. FMOD's spatial audio layering makes this achievable but requires careful mix work.

04
Scholar's Murmur System
The Scholar's academic instinct surfaces as near-inaudible Avestan recognition — text objects only
ORIGIN · TDS deep-read VO (extended) · no direct game precedent
TIER · Level 3A

KENOMA already has a Scholar murmur system for deep-reading: the TDS Controller fires VO lines as the Scholar translates at depth. This design extends murmur earlier — into the approach and recognition phase before interaction is initiated.

When the Scholar enters proximity of a text-bearing object (tablet, frieze, inscription), they murmur a single word or short phrase in Avestan under their breath. Not the translation — the recognition. "Old Elamite." "Founding script." "Her hand." These are the Scholar's academic classification instincts firing automatically, in the Scholar's private analytical voice, barely audible.

This system is exclusive to text objects — the most important interactable category in KENOMA. Mechanisms, sealed doors, and physical objects do not produce murmur signals (they produce flame sympathy or head-turn instead). The Scholar is a scholar. Their first response to an inscription is always recognition. This is character behaviour, not a game mechanic.

The Dark Voice is already implemented as a binaural spatial audio system (Behind / Beside / Ahead). The murmur system uses the same infrastructure but fires from a different point: directly from the Scholar's mouth position, no spatial processing. The distinction: the Dark Voice speaks from inside the skull; the murmur comes from the mouth. Players with headphones should feel the difference physically — one voice is behind them, one is in front of them.

The critical limitation: this system only works for players with audio on at audible levels, and only for text objects. It supplements Designs 01 and 02 for the most important KENOMA objects but cannot stand alone as a complete solution.

05
Composed Camera-Object Priority
The AitD 1 origin principle — elevated to an explicit authoring rule for KENOMA's fixed camera system
ORIGIN · AitD 1 (foundational) · Metro (environmental)
TIER · Level 1 — Compositional

Every fixed camera in AitD 1 was composed so that the most important interactive object in the room was visible in frame when the player entered. This was not an accident — it was the designer's substitute for an indicator system. KENOMA must elevate this from implicit convention to explicit authoring law.

The rule: every camera in a room where a critical interactable exists must show the Scholar, the interactable, and at minimum the Vanguard's position simultaneously. The KENOMA scene review checklist already contains this requirement (Scene Review §08). This design codifies its interaction-indicator function.

The implementation requires a camera suitability check at review time for every room: Does camera position X show the Scholar near the critical tablet? Yes → approved. Does it show only the Scholar walking toward a wall? No → flag for revision. This is not a runtime system — it is a production pipeline constraint.

The secondary tool: lighting composition. In World A, fire altars are placed so that their warmth falls on critical objects, slightly illuminating them relative to their surroundings. Not a spotlight — a slight, ambient directional preference. The lighting designer is told: this tablet must be warmer than the surrounding stone in the primary camera angle. In World B, the Scholar's fire vessel naturally illuminates their immediate surroundings; positioning interactable objects within the fire vessel's ambient radius — visible but not obviously spotlighted — is the compositional tool.

This design is the foundation layer beneath all others. Designs 01–04 supplement this; they cannot replace it. A room with excellent body posture signaling (Design 01) but poor camera composition will still leave players confused. A room with excellent camera composition and no supplementary signal is playable — as AitD 1 demonstrated in 1992.

Verdict & Recommended Stack

The Indicator Stack for KENOMA

No single mechanism solves all cases. The correct approach is a layered stack where each design handles a different detection scenario, and all five reinforce each other without redundancy. The stack is ordered by priority: failure at layer 1 is never compensated by layer 5.

01 · Scholar's Gaze — always-on foundation for characters 05 · Camera Composition — always-on foundation for rooms 02 · Fire Vessel Sympathy — directional aid in all lighting 03 · Astrolabe Resonance — proximity audio layer 04 · Scholar Murmur — text-object character layer

Designs 01 and 05 are mandatory. The Scholar's gaze architecture and camera composition are the structural substrate — they must be implemented first, reviewed at every scene-review checkpoint, and never compromised for other production reasons. These are not optional enhancements; they are the iron floor of KENOMA's zero-HUD interaction contract.

Design 02 is mandatory for World B. In World B's near-darkness, the flame sympathy becomes the primary spatial navigation tool. A flame leaning into darkness toward a cold tablet that has not been touched in 300 years is one of the most characteristically KENOMA moments the game can produce — an intimate communication between the Scholar's warmth and the Archive's silence. This should be implemented alongside the lighting design for World B from day one.

Designs 03 and 04 are enhancement layers. The astrolabe resonance mode adds a second channel for players who are listening carefully and have learned the system. The scholar murmur adds character texture and world-building to the most important interactable type. Both are zero-HUD compliant and both reward attentive players — but neither is needed for the base interaction contract to function.

The critical divergence from Alone in the Dark's original model: AitD 1 used camera composition as its only indicator and trusted that the player would try things. KENOMA cannot rely on that trust alone because its BMS-significant objects are specifically those that require deep-reading — a 25-second commitment. A player who misses a tablet doesn't get an alternative path to the same BMS consequence. The murmur system and flame sympathy exist precisely to catch the player who would otherwise walk past the most important objects in the room without knowing they were important.

The AitD: New Nightmare flashlight insight remains the most elegant single mechanic in the genre for this problem — a light source that already exists, already serves combat and navigation, and whose orientation toward an object carries information for free. Design 02 is KENOMA's direct descendant of that insight, adapted for an always-carried ambient light rather than a player-aimed beam. The Scholar does not aim the fire vessel. The fire vessel responds to the Archive. That distinction is the game's thesis expressed as a UX decision.

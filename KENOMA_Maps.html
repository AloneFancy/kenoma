§ 01 Tormented Souls 1 & 2 — Gameplay & Map Structure Analysis Navigation · Camera · Backtracking · World Logic

Core Camera & Navigation System — Shared DNA

Both games inherit the classic survival horror fixed-camera model: each room triggers a pre-set camera angle that does not follow the player. This is not a technical limitation — it is a deliberate spatial storytelling tool. The camera decides what you see and what you don't. A room's angle can hide a creature, reveal a resource, or frame a door in a way that tells the player it matters before they reach it.

Tank controls (relative to character, not camera) are retained in both games. This creates intentional friction in dangerous rooms — the player cannot instantly reverse course, cannot circle-strafe, must commit to movement decisions. In TS1, this friction is felt inside the mansion's narrow corridors. In TS2, it is felt across wider zone spaces with more complex geometry. The friction is designed as tension calibration, not difficulty from poor UX.

Tormented Souls 1 · 2021 The Spine Model — One Building, Layered Vertically
Structure: Single mansion building carved into multiple functional zones (Ground Floor, Basement, Upper Floor, Bunker, Roof). All zones are physically connected — no loading-screen "travel" between areas. The player navigates a continuous building.

Navigation logic: A central spine (main hallway, stairwells, the elevator shaft) connects all floors. Side rooms branch off the spine. The player always knows approximately where they are relative to the building's center because the spine is consistently accessible.

Backtracking: Moderate and intentional. Most keys unlock doors within 1-3 rooms. The mansion is compact enough that a player holding an unused item is never more than 3 minutes from its destination. The mirror crossings add a vertical layer — the "corrupted world" is accessed from fixed mirror points on the main floor and basement, creating a predictable parallel navigation plane.

Camera philosophy: Fixed angles are pre-rendered — high visual detail, consistent framing, every angle was art-directed. Camera positions often reveal story: the maternity ward's camera angle includes the shifted patient before the player approaches, rewarding attention. The operating theater camera is from above — the surgeon's perspective on the subject.
Tormented Souls 2 · 2025 The Zone Model — Multiple Locations, Lateral Scale
Structure: Seven distinct zones (Convent, Prison, School, Cemetery, Town, Processing Plant, Church/Bunker), each a separate large area connected by specific transit points. Some zones require backtracking through previously visited zones to progress in a later one.

Navigation logic: Each zone has its own internal loop structure — areas designed to reconnect and allow efficient movement after the player learns them. The zone connections are linear (Convent → Prison → School → Cemetery → Town → Plant → Church) but zones open backward for key retrieval. The in-game map becomes essential — TS1 rarely needed it.

Backtracking: More extensive and occasionally frustrating. Key items for one zone are sometimes found in a previous zone (a document in the Convent needed for a Cemetery puzzle). The expanded scale means "I need to go back" can cost 4-6 minutes of travel. The "Other Side" crossing adds another layer in each zone — a parallel version of the same space, not a separate building.

Camera philosophy: Real-time 3D cameras (not pre-rendered) for most spaces, with fixed-angle triggers for specific corridors and rooms. The visual detail is higher, but the art-direction-per-angle is less precise than TS1's. Caroline's examination comments (press button near objects) replace some of the story-through-camera-angle that TS1 achieved architecturally.

KENOMA's Position KENOMA takes TS1's single-location discipline and TS2's depth-of-world ambition simultaneously. One Archive building — but with two complete worlds inside it, each with its own philosophical puzzle vocabulary, its own creature ecology, and its own Vanguard. The variety TS2 generated through seven geographic zones, KENOMA generates through civilizational divergence within the same physical structure. The corridor you cross in World A is the same corridor — inverted — in World B. The player learns both at once.

§ 04 From Analysis to Architecture — What TS1 & TS2 Teach About the KENOMA Map

What TS1's Spine Model Gives KENOMA

TS1's greatest navigational achievement: players always know where they are relative to the main spine (elevator, main hallway). The fixed cameras are positioned to reinforce this — they don't just frame a scene, they orient the player. A camera angled down the main hall toward a door communicates "that door matters, and you know how to get back to where you are." KENOMA's Hypostyle Forest (WA-002 / WB-002 in its mirror) serves the same function: it is the widest, most recognizable space, and every other room connects to it visibly. Players return to the Hypostyle the way TS1 players return to the main hallway — it is the cognitive anchor.

KENOMA Adoption from TS1 The Hypostyle Forest's 72 columns are not decoration — they are a navigation system. After the Scholar lights fire altars, the Hypostyle becomes unmistakable: lit columns stretch in both directions, the processional scale tells you exactly how far from the Gate you are. This is TS1's corridor-as-landmark, applied at Persepolis scale.

What TS2's Zone Depth Teaches KENOMA About World B

TS2's most praised level design is the Processing Plant — a space that loops back on itself, reveals new areas on revisit, and builds a mental model incrementally. The Dark Archive (World B) must achieve the same density of revisit-reward, but without the luxury of distinct visual zone identities. World B's identity is sameness-with-wrongness: the same column grid, but columns hang from the ceiling. The same carved friezes, but the procession has gone wrong mid-journey. The player navigates by learning World A perfectly and then reading the Dark Archive as its distorted reflection. Navigation in World B is not spatial — it is mnemonic. You find your way because you remember where this is in World A, and then you apply the inversion logic.

KENOMA's Answer to TS2's Zone-Variety Problem Two philosophical puzzle vocabularies = two distinct navigational languages in one building. Sacred Fire logic tells you where valuable tablets are (in restricted positions). Hollow logic tells you the same information inverted (in apparently accessible positions). The player develops two navigation modes — one per world — without needing seven geographic zones to generate variety.

The Interference Puzzle Locations — Why These Positions

Scenario A (Central Courtyard): Open sky. A crossing-junction accessible to both Vanguards naturally — the courtyard is on the path between the Hypostyle (main hub) and the Eastern Treasury (puzzle hub). Both Vanguards pass through it routinely. The well's calendar-marker implies both worlds to a Scholar who reads carefully. Its open-air setting means the Scholar cannot control who sees them working.

Scenario B (Eastern Alcove): Removed from the main traffic flow, but acoustically connected — sound travels through the Hypostyle's stone columns. The Resonance Obelisk's failure mode (destructive acoustic interference) fits perfectly in a space where sound travels between worlds. The Keeper Designation's two-line revelation is most effective here because the Scholar is alone with it — no immediate pressure, just time with an inscription that implies the entire Archive's dual nature.

Scenario C (Inner Courtyard, Crossing Junction): Placed at the Archive's crossing junction — the most overlap-dense point between worlds. The Still-Water Oracle's 300-year failure (both Vanguards unknowingly interfering) is also the most compressed statement of the game's central tragedy. Placing it at the literal crossing point between worlds makes the story and the architecture say the same thing simultaneously.

Scenario D (Great Hall): The game's widest indoor space — visible from the Gallery above, accessible from the Processional Corridor, the Treasury, and the Courtyard. Placing the Translation Obelisk (the most socially complex interference puzzle) here means the Scholar manages a command conflict in the space where anyone can observe them. The Foundation Record's Clause 18 is the Archive's legal completion trigger — it belongs in the most formal administrative space.
